# User Interview Template

A reusable script for problem-discovery interviews. Use this as a guide, not as a script.

---

## Before the interview

- **Who you're talking to:** _[describe the person and why they fit your target user]_
- **What you want to learn:** _[the one or two things this conversation needs to answer]_
- **Riskiest assumption you're testing:** _[the belief that, if wrong, changes everything]_

---

## Opening (2 minutes)

Set them at ease and explain there are no wrong answers.

> "Thanks for taking the time. I'm trying to understand how people deal with [problem area], so I'm just going to ask about your own experience. There are no right or wrong answers, I'm here to learn from you. I'll be taking notes."

---

## Warm-up (3–5 minutes)

Easy, factual questions to get them talking.

- "Tell me a bit about your day-to-day when it comes to [relevant area]."
- "How do you currently handle [task or problem]?"

---

## The core: ask about the past

Get real stories, not predictions. Anchor every question to something that actually happened.

- "Tell me about the last time you tried to [do the thing]."
- "Walk me through what happened, start to finish."
- "What were you using to do it? Why that?"
- "What happened next?"
- "Where did it get frustrating, if it did?"
- "Why did you stop?" _(if they stopped)_
- "Was there a moment it felt like it was working? What was that like?"

---

## Follow the surprise

When something unexpected comes up, leave the script and dig in.

- "That's interesting, tell me more about that."
- "Why do you think that is?"
- "Can you give me an example?"
- "What did you do then?"

---

## Avoid these (leading questions)

Do not ask questions that plant your assumption or invite a polite guess about the future:

- ~~"Would you use a [your product]?"~~
- ~~"Don't you find [problem] frustrating?"~~
- ~~"How much would [your feature] help you?"~~

Replace each with a question about what they have actually done.

---

## Closing (2 minutes)

- "Is there anything about this I should have asked but didn't?"
- "Is there anyone else you'd suggest I talk to?"
- "Thank them. Ask if you can follow up."

---

## After the interview (do this immediately)

- **3 things they actually said** _(in their own words, as close to verbatim as you can)_
- **1 thing that surprised you or contradicted your expectation**
- **What this means for your riskiest assumption:** _[did it hold, weaken, or break?]_

---

## Reminders

- Ask about the past, not the future. People recount better than they predict.
- Ask open questions. "What happened next?" opens a story; "Was it frustrating?" closes it.
- Stay quiet. Silence makes people fill the space with the useful stuff. If they go quiet, ask them to think out loud.
- Use the script to stay honest and on track, but go off it when a surprise is worth chasing.
