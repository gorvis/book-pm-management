## Part 1 — The Venn

Place your decision against all three lenses. Write one sentence for each.

**Business:** how does this decision serve the business?

**Customer:** what customer need does it address?

**Delivery:** is it feasible to build with the resources available?

> If any lens fails, stop. Revise the decision or pick a different one.

---

## Part 2 — Mission to Results stack

Trace your decision up the stack. Fill in each level.

**Key result:** how will you measure whether this worked?

**Objective:** what is the team focused on that this supports?

**Strategy:** what strategic direction does this serve?

**Vision:** where is Pulse going that makes this relevant?

**Mission:** why does Pulse exist?

> The line has to hold all the way up. If it breaks at any level, note where and why.

---

## Part 3 — Discovery hypothesis

Before anything gets built, you need a hypothesis. This is what separates strategic product work from random building — a hypothesis gives you something to come back to after you ship and measure. Without it, you can't know whether what you learned was what you were trying to find out.

This is where the Learn → Build → Measure loop starts. You're not in the Build phase yet. You're forming the belief you're about to test.

Write your hypothesis in this format:

> We believe [type of user] has a problem with [specific situation]. We think [this feature or change] will [produce this outcome]. We will know we are right when [measurable signal].

**Your hypothesis:**

> We believe \_\_\_ has a problem with \_\_\_. We think \_\_\_ will \_\_\_. We will know we are right when \_\_\_.

> Keep it falsifiable. If there's no way to be wrong, it's not a hypothesis — it's a wish.
